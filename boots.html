Football Boots
History

As amazing as it sounds, the first pair of football boots ever recorded were made for king Henry VIII in 1526.

Boot design and evolution could be said to have started during the 1800s, when players currently wearing steel-toe capped, long-laced work-boots, worn more for protection than anything else, added metal studs or tacks to the soles of their boots to add extra grip and better traction.

By the 1900s boots were being manufactured by known names such as Gola, Hummel and Valsport, but up until the 1940s, boot design changed very little.

Some say it was the influence of South American players, who played on flatter, drier pitches than their European counterparts that demanded boots offering more comfort and control, sacrificing the bulk and protection of current boots, for lighter, lower-cut boots.

In 1954, Adi Dassler (founder of Adidas) and his brother Rudi (founder of Puma) designed the first ever boots with screw-in studs. The German national team exclusively wore these boots to devastating effect to win a rain-lashed World Cup that year.

The 60s and 70s saw boots adapt to aid players to be quicker and change direction faster. Mitre, Joma and Asics joined the boot making market. The late 70s saw the arrival of classic boots such as Puma’s King range, and the best selling boot of all time, Adidas’s Copa Mondial.

The 80s onwards saw the likes of Umbro, Lotto and Kelme entered the boot making fray. Boot sponsorship pushed up boot sales, and revenue drives innovation.

The 90s saw Adidas revolutionise the boot world once again, when in 1994 they released their Predator range, designed by ex-Liverpool player Craig Johnstone.

At the turn of the century, laser technology was introduced to the boot manufacturing process, leading to the first fully customisable boot in 2006.

Today's modern boots are made by multi-million pound multi-national companies at the forefront of technology and innovation using cutting-edge designs and materials.

Football Boots
Types & Choices

Modern boots are classed as:

  • SG or Soft ground
  • FG or Firm ground
  • Astroturf boots/shoes
  • Indoor boots/shoes

SG or soft ground boots will have either all fully interchangeable studs, or have a combination of screw-in and moulded/bladed soles. Having interchangeable studs enable the player to adjust individual stud length depending on playing conditions.

FG or firm ground boots will be either moulded or bladed studs, or a combination of both. FG boots are made for firmer, drier grass surfaces and astroturf/4g surfaces.

Astroturf boots have multiple smaller studs on the sole to aid grip, and made primarily for astroturf/4g surfaces.

Indoor boots or shoes have flat rubber soles exclusively for indoor surfaces and gymnasiums.

Boot choice is primarily a personal choice after taking into consideration the playing surface. Be especially weary of grass surfaces when its very hot and dry, and cold and frosty mornings as frequently used, untended surfaces can be very uneven.

Your playing position may also influence your choice of boot, an attacking player or striker may favour a lighter boot to maximise acceleration and changes of direction. A midfielder may prefer a boot offering more comfort and control, and a defender may want a boot offering a bit more protection and a clean strike of the ball.

Whichever boots you choose, ensure they fit well, they must obviously be comfortable, and the best choice for the surface you are playing on.
