HYDRATION

Good hydration is pretty simple for human beings, we have a fail-safe built-in mechanism called thirst, that tells us when we feel dehydrated, when this fail-safe kicks in, we drink something.

As players, we should understand the effects of hydration and de-hydration on the human body, and how to prepare ourselves before games, and for recovery afterwards.

How strong we feel, how we perform, and for how long, and how we feel and recover afterwards are all affected by fluid intake and what we have eaten, and when.

Good hydration principles basically means getting enough fluids into the body before, during and after sessions or games.

It is widely acknowledged in the sporting world that dehydration increases cardiovascular strain, thermal strain, and glycogen utilisation in turn depleting stores in the body, muscle lactate production (contributing to muscle fatigue) and perceived rate of exertion.

What this means is:

  • hydrated muscles function better than dehydrated muscles
  • staying hydrated maintains blood pressure, reducing strain to the heart
  • good hydration improves blood flow and circulation thus increasing the delivery of energy and nutrients to the working muscles
  • good hydration removes metabolic by-products and waste from muscles, lessening fatigue
  • it also replaces fluids lost through sweating

For young players, hydration with water is sufficient, sports drinks can be used if the game or session is particularly intense, or the conditions are very hot and humid.
Experts recommend a sports drink containing 110-240mg of sodium per 8oz serving (approx 1 glass)

PRE-GAME

Players should be aiming to drink approx 500ml of water/fluids from 2hrs before the game or session, then 125-150ml immediately beforehand

SESSIONS

During a training session a player should be aiming to drink 125-150ml every 15-20mins

POST-GAME

After a session or game you should aim to consume 150% of the amount of fluid lost.

This does not have to be done immediately or all at once.

This is a basic introduction to hydration, for more comprehensive information, please visit The British Nutrition Foundation Hydration Guide.
